## The Suspicion of Boring Solutions

I think the internet has made us weirdly suspicious of boring solutions.

Not old solutions, necessarily. Just boring ones.

A notebook. A spreadsheet. A torque wrench. Ethernet. An external hard drive that lives on a shelf and only wakes up once every couple of weeks to hold backups before going back to sleep again like some sort of digital bear preparing for winter.

None of these things make particularly good YouTube videos, which may actually be part of the problem. Nobody is building an entire online brand around the revolutionary concept of writing things down in a small paper book and then continuing to write things down in that same small paper book until the problem eventually goes away. There are no Reddit wars over external hard drives. Nobody is filming cinematic B-roll of a torque wrench clicking correctly. No startup founder has ever walked onto a stage in a black turtleneck and announced that they have finally disrupted the concept of plugging in an ethernet cable and immediately getting reliable internet.

Meanwhile there are entire industries dedicated to replacing all of these things with something newer, smarter, prettier, more connected, and usually subscription-based.

Apparently the main flaw with notebooks was that they stubbornly refused to charge $11.99 per month.

## The Spreadsheet Problem

There is an old joke that every sufficiently complicated organization eventually recreates Excel badly, and every year I become more convinced that this is less of a joke and more of an observed law of nature.

People spend millions of dollars building project management platforms, inventory systems, telemetry dashboards, AI knowledge bases, workflow automation suites, and data ecosystems that eventually arrive at the startling conclusion that maybe information could be represented as rows and columns.

Which, incidentally, is a spreadsheet.

Sometimes it has dark mode. Sometimes it has rounded corners. Sometimes it has AI features that are apparently incapable of answering the question "why are these numbers wrong?" but are very capable of generating a watercolor image of your quarterly sales figures as a woodland creature. Sometimes it has a pricing structure that requires speaking to a sales representative and scheduling a discovery call.

But a spreadsheet nonetheless.

And the irritating thing is that sometimes the spreadsheet actually was the correct answer all along.

Not because spreadsheets are exciting, because they aren't. They are many things, but exciting is not one of them. They're legible, though. You can see the numbers. You can see the assumptions. If something looks wrong, you can usually trace the problem back to where it started. If somebody hands you a spreadsheet they built three years ago, there is at least a reasonable chance that after half an hour of muttering under your breath and opening increasingly obscure tabs you'll understand what they were trying to accomplish.

Try doing that with enterprise software.

Especially enterprise software that was acquired by another company that was later acquired by a holding company whose primary business activity appears to be acquiring software and making the logo flatter.

## The Verbatim Lesson

Actually, I think I learned some version of this lesson in debate.

For people lucky enough to have avoided high school policy debate, evidence management used to be a fairly chaotic combination of Word documents, folder trees, USB drives, and file naming conventions that ranged from "reasonably organized" to "this should probably qualify as evidence in a criminal investigation."

Then came Verbatim.

And Verbatim was supposed to fix all of it.

Integrated evidence management. Automatic citations. Cleaner speech documents. Better prep organization. Easier sharing. More professional-looking files. Less friction. Less chaos.

And to be fair to it, it genuinely did all of those things.

Our files looked incredible. Everything looked cleaner, more modern, more serious. If someone had walked past our computers during a tournament and glanced at our prep, they probably would have thought, "these people absolutely know what they're doing."

The weird part is that I slowly started realizing I was actually debating worse.

Not catastrophically worse. Not "drop five rounds in a row and begin researching trade schools during lunch" worse. Just slightly worse in ways that were difficult to measure and even harder to explain.

I was spending more time managing systems and less time understanding arguments. More time organizing evidence and less time reading evidence. More time making prep look impressive and less time asking whether the prep was actually good.

The software optimized for feeling prepared in a way that occasionally made me less prepared.

Which turns out to be a surprisingly important distinction.

There is an entire category of technology whose primary purpose is making you feel like the sort of person who definitely has their life together. Sometimes that's useful. Sometimes it's productivity theater with synchronization features.

## The Maintenance Trap

Photography has this problem constantly.

It is unbelievably easy to convince yourself that your issue is a camera problem when it is actually a lighting problem, or a composition problem, or a patience problem. Motorsport people do exactly the same thing with suspension parts and horsepower. Developers do it with frameworks. Homelab people do it with hardware. Linux users do it with infrastructure.

Actually, Linux users might be the worst offenders here.

There are people running enough redundant services to maintain regional air traffic control operations whose primary daily computing tasks consist of opening Firefox and checking email.

Every hobby eventually develops its own version of the same thought:

"If I just buy the next thing, then I'll finally be able to do the thing I wanted to do in the first place."

Sometimes that's true.

Sometimes you've just purchased a new category of maintenance.

The additional server needs updates and backups and monitoring and replacement drives and suddenly you've spent an entire Saturday trying to determine why one container refuses to communicate with another container across a VLAN that you created specifically because you were trying to simplify your setup.

The additional camera body needs batteries and chargers and accessories and sensor cleanings and eventually repairs.

The additional race car needs tires and bearings and fluids and trailer space and storage and all of the invisible costs that quietly attach themselves to objects once those objects enter your life.

The purchase price is often the cheapest part of ownership.

Everything after that is a subscription service run by reality itself.

## What This Means For People

I think this matters outside of technology because the exact same instinct shows up everywhere else.

We assume that if a solution feels too simple then we probably haven't found the real answer yet. Surely there must be another layer to it. Surely somebody smarter has already discovered the complicated version and we just haven't encountered it yet.

If your finances are a mess, surely the answer is a budgeting platform with predictive analytics, machine learning categorization, bank integrations, and enough dashboards to launch a satellite.

It would be deeply inconvenient if the answer turned out to be "look at your bank account more often."

If you're struggling to stay organized, surely the answer is a second-brain system involving bidirectional links, graph views, knowledge management methodologies, and enough jargon to accidentally qualify as a religious movement.

It would be mildly embarrassing if the answer was a notebook.

If you're overwhelmed at work, surely the answer is a productivity framework developed by consultants with matching headshots and a trademark symbol somewhere in the title.

It would be awkward if the answer was simply deciding what matters and doing that first.

Complicated solutions feel smarter. They feel more professional. More serious. More deserving of respect.

Simple solutions feel temporary. Amateur. Incomplete.

People will proudly explain a seven-stage workflow involving four applications and an API integration. Nobody wants to stand up and announce that they solved the problem with a legal pad and a pen stolen from a hotel lobby.

And yet those people continue succeeding at deeply irritating rates.

I think part of becoming competent at almost anything is learning to ask a slightly uncomfortable question:

Am I solving the problem, or am I purchasing a more interesting version of the problem?

Those are not always the same thing.

Sometimes they aren't even particularly close.

## When New Things Actually Are Better

And this isn't an argument against new things.

Some new things are genuinely better.

Fuel injection is better than carburetors.

SSDs are better than spinning disks for most people.

Modern insulin pumps are better than what came before them by a margin that is honestly difficult to overstate.

Sometimes progress is just progress.

The interesting thing is that actual progress usually doesn't spend very much time explaining why it exists. Nobody has to create thought leadership content explaining why power steering caught on. Nobody has to write Medium articles defending antibiotics.

When something is dramatically better, reality tends to do most of the marketing work.

## The Question Worth Asking

The trick isn't rejecting new things.

The trick is figuring out whether the boring solution is boring because it lost twenty years ago or whether it's boring because it won twenty years ago and nobody has actually managed to beat it since.

Those are very different situations that often look identical from a distance.

Because every industry has incentives to sell you innovation.

Reality remains stubbornly uninterested in whether the solution was innovative.

Reality only cares whether the thing works.

Which is unfortunate news for an awful lot of marketing departments.
